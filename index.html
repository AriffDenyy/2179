Sports & Fitness in Malaysia -
Where People Work Out and How

Malaysia has thousands of sports and fitness venues spread across its 16 states. But where are they concentrated, what types dominate, and who actually uses them? This visualisation tells that story - from the national map all the way to the gym floor.

Section 1
The landscape - where are the venues?

Sports and fitness venues are not evenly distributed across Malaysia. Peninsular Malaysia - especially the Klang Valley - is home to the vast majority, while East Malaysia (Sarawak and Sabah) remains comparatively underserved. Use the map controls to zoom into each state and explore venue density by category.

Key insight: Selangor leads with 349 venues - more than double the next highest state - reflecting the concentration of urban population and commercial activity around the Klang Valley.
Now that we know where venues are - what kinds of facilities do Malaysians actually have access to?
Section 2
What's available - types of venues

Gyms & Studios dominate the venue landscape with 366 facilities nationally, followed by Fishing & Hunting and Recreation Centers. Niche categories like Paintball & Laser and Snow & Winter Sports exist but remain rare. The radar chart reveals that Golf and Skating demand the most time - over 2 hours on average per visit.

Key insight: Despite gyms being the most numerous venue type, Fishing & Hunting ranks second - a reminder that outdoor recreation remains deeply embedded in Malaysian leisure culture.
Quantity is one thing - but are these venues actually good? Let's look at what customers say.
Section 3
Quality of experience - how venues are rated

Customer ratings across all 15 venue categories cluster tightly between 4.0 and 4.5 stars, suggesting consistent quality regardless of venue type. Martial Arts & Dojos and Racquet & Club Sports show the tightest distributions, while Gyms & Studios display more variability - not all gyms are created equal.

Key insight: No category averages below 4.0, suggesting Malaysians have access to genuinely good facilities. The outliers worth watching are individual low-rated venues pulling down the floor of otherwise strong categories.
We've seen the venues from the outside. Now let's step inside - who are the people actually working out?
Section 4
Inside the gym - who works out and how

Strength training and Cardio are the dominant workout types, together accounting for over 60% of gym activity. Younger members (20-35) gravitate toward high-intensity formats, while older age groups favour Yoga and steady Cardio. Beginners tend to train 2-3 days a week; advanced members push to 4-5 days.

Key insight: The 40-50 age group has the highest total member count - suggesting mid-life health awareness is a key driver of gym attendance in Malaysia.
Finally - what does the health profile of gym-goers look like, and does working harder actually make a difference?
Section 5
Fitness outcomes - BMI, calories and experience

Advanced gym members burn significantly more calories per session, regardless of BMI. Most gym-goers fall within the Normal BMI range, but a notable share are Overweight or Obese - indicating that gyms are actively serving people on a health improvement journey, not just those who are already fit.

Key insight: BMI alone doesn't predict calorie burn - experience level does. An advanced member with a high BMI consistently outperforms a beginner with a low one, reinforcing the value of consistency over starting point.
